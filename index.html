Abstract

This study measures how websites implement and communicate consent revocation (the right to withdraw consent under the GDPR) on popular websites. We audited Tranco’s top-200 sites and CMPs (IAB TCF and OneTrust) to check whether revocation is available, correctly recorded, and signalled to third parties.

Key takeaways
  • Many sites make revocation harder than granting consent — a legal and usability problem.
  • Revocation is often not persisted correctly, and third-parties are rarely informed.
  • Regulators and CMPs should standardize revocation signalling and storage.
Methodology

Automated and manual audit of Tranco top-200 websites, and deeper analysis of 281 sites using IAB TCF or OneTrust CMPs to test storage and third-party communication.

Recommendations
  1. Make revocation as visible and as easy as acceptance (single step).
  2. Standardize revocation storage formats & APIs so third-parties can be reliably informed.
  3. Require CMPs to implement server-side notifications to third-parties when revocation happens.

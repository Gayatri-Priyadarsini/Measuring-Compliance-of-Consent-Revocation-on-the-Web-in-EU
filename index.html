Measuring Compliance of Consent Revocation on the Web

Presented at Privacy Enhancing Technologies Symposium (PETS) 2025 — Washington DC, USA
Gayatri P. Kancherla - IIT Gandhinagar, India
Nataliia Bielova - Inria Centre at Univ Côte d’Azur, France
Cristiana Santos - Utrecht University, Netherlands
Abhishek Bichhawat - IIT Gandhinagar, India
Summary

The GDPR and ePrivacy Directive guarantee users the right to withdraw consent at any time. Yet, little is known about how websites implement this in practice. This paper examines 200 popular websites and their Consent Management Platforms (CMPs) — specifically IAB Europe’s Transparency and Consent Framework (TCF) and OneTrust — to assess whether they offer compliant and functional ways to revoke consent.

Our study found that nearly half of the websites do not comply with legal and technical standards for revocation. Many either hide the revocation option or make it harder to withdraw consent than to give it. Even when users revoke consent, websites frequently fail to update stored records or notify third parties. Consequently, advertising and analytics cookies often remain active even after consent has been revoked.

Detailed Violations and Examples

1. Different Interface to Revoke Consent

According to GDPR, revocation must be as easy as giving consent and accessible through the same interface. However, 20% of websites use completely different mechanisms. We observed revocation options that require users to delete cookies from the browser settings, contact website owners via email, or redirect the user to third-party platforms, such as youronlinechoices.eu or aboutads.info. One website, tumblr.com, even required users to log in before they could revoke their consent. These violations are found on popular websites such as apple.com, wordpress.org, medium.com, and discord.com.

Example: On wordpress.com, users must open “Privacy Policy → Cookies” and manually delete cookies through browser settings — an approach that fails accessibility and simplicity standards.

2. More Effort to Revoke than Accept

22% of websites require multiple steps to revoke consent compared to a single click for “Accept all.” For example, google.com and twitter.com require navigating through several pages to reopen the banner.

Example: On goo.gl, revoking consent took seven steps — starting from Privacy Policy navigation to finding a link buried under “cookies".

3. Absence of a Revocation Mechanism

6% of websites, including un.org and weibo.com, provided no revocation option at all. Such sites continue to collect analytics and advertising data, violating the GDPR’s lawfulness principle.

4. Incorrect Storage of Revocation

We found that 12–15% of CMP-based sites store a positive consent even after the user revokes it. This issue was frequent on msn.com, cisco.com, and forbes.com.

5. No Notification to Third Parties

Revocation was rarely communicated to third parties. 74% of websites failed to notify all parties who received the initial consent signal. This means ad networks like cnn.com, bbc.com, and wsj.com continued to receive consented data.

6. Cookies Persist After Revocation

57% of sites continued to store advertising and analytics cookies post-revocation. Popular offenders include microsoft.com, twitter.com, and amazon.com.

Example: After revocation, youtube.com still retained YSC and VISITOR_INFO1_LIVE cookies — both used for analytics and ad personalization.

Recommendations

In this section, we propose recommendations to regulators for enhancing the implementation of consent revocation and ensuring legal compliance, in light of our findings.

  1. R1. Unify EU consent revocation interface:

    Websites implement revocation inconsistently (see Table 4). Texts in footers vary (“Cookie Settings”, “Privacy Settings”, “EU Privacy”), and revocation links in privacy policies may be unclear (“How do I control cookies...”). Icons also differ, causing confusion. Regulators should define unified interface requirements for location, wording, and appearance. Inspiration can be taken from CPRA regulation in California with three acceptable wordings to simplify compliance measurement.

  2. R2. Standardize consent storage:

    Observed inconsistencies in TCStrings and OneTrust consent strings occur because multiple storage options are not updated simultaneously on revocation. Regulators should establish standards for consent storage and ensure integrity. Under SOP, third-party scripts can access first-party storage, potentially modifying consent strings, which may invalidate consent and violate GDPR Article 5(f).

  3. R3. Standardize consent communication via event listeners:

    Only 43/163 websites used event listeners to inform third-parties of consent changes. OneTrust suggests adding them, but IAB Europe TCF lacks standardization, forcing CMPs to implement their own solutions. Standardization of event listeners and callbacks is needed, along with regulatory guidance on communication to third-parties.

  4. R4. Regulate consent communication via HTTP requests:

    The most common violation: 74.2% of websites fail to inform all third-parties via HTTP requests when consent is revoked. Regulators should define clear standards and best practices for these communications.

Methodology

We audited 200 popular websites, manually analyzed revocation UI/UX, and used automated scripts to observe how consent decisions were stored and transmitted. CMP-specific storage was evaluated using TCString and OneTrust’s OptanonConsent cookies.

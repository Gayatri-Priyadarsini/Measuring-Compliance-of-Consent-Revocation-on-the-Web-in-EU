Measuring Usability and Compliance of Consent Revocation

Findings from our semi-automated study of cookie banners across regions

Summary

Our study analyzed 158 websites to assess whether consent revocation complies with GDPR and ePrivacy Directive requirements. We examined the ease of revocation, the consistency of interfaces, and whether consent withdrawal truly stops tracking. Four major usability and compliance issues were identified.

1. Different Interface to Revoke Consent

Legal Expectation:

Revoking consent should be as easy and accessible as giving it, ideally through the same interface (e.g., the banner).

Result:

About 20% of websites (32/158) provide a different or hidden interface for revocation, often requiring users to revisit privacy policies or find secondary links.

Example:

WordPress offers a separate “Cookie Settings” page for revocation instead of allowing users to modify choices via the original banner.

2. More Effort Required to Revoke Consent

Legal Expectation:

The effort to withdraw consent must not exceed that of giving consent (Article 7(3) GDPR).

Result:

40% of websites require additional steps for revocation — such as navigating multiple menus, clearing cookies, or clicking through several links.

Example:

Google requires users to access multiple submenus to change cookie preferences, whereas giving consent takes only one click on the banner.

3. Storage & Communication Issues

Result:

In 15% of cases, cookie preference storage was unreliable — settings reverted after reload or were stored locally without server synchronization, potentially losing user preferences.

4. Tracking Continues After Revocation

Result:

About 10% of tested websites continued to load tracking scripts even after consent was revoked, violating the GDPR requirement for valid consent withdrawal.

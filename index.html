Measuring Compliance of Consent Revocation on the Web

Our study analyzed popular websites to assess whether consent revocation mechanisms comply with GDPR and ePrivacy Directive requirements. Widespread non-compliance was observed in interface design, consent storage, and tracking behavior.

1. Different Interface to Revoke Consent

Legal Expectation:

Revoking consent should be as easy as giving it, preferably through the same interface (e.g., the banner).

Result:

20% of websites (32/158) provide a separate or hidden interface, requiring extra effort from users.

Example:

WordPress: Users must go to a separate “Cookie Settings” page to revoke consent instead of using the original banner interface.

2. More Effort to Revoke Consent

Legal Expectation:

Revoking consent must require no more steps than giving consent.

Result:

22% of websites (35/158) require more steps to revoke consent, creating usability friction.

Example:

Google: Multiple nested menus must be navigated to revoke consent, whereas granting consent is a single click on the banner.

3. Storage & Communication Issues

Result:

15% of websites store revocation inconsistently, fail to update consent storage, or do not communicate revocation to all third parties.

4. Tracking Continues After Revocation

Result:

57% of websites continue to store tracking cookies even after consent has been revoked, violating GDPR requirements.

Key Findings

  • 49% of websites offer non-compliant revocation interfaces
  • 66% fail to store or communicate consent revocation correctly
  • 57% continue to store tracking cookies after consent revocation
